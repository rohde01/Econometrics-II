## 1. Introduction

- Financial investors are typically interested in both **mean** and **variance**. This reflects the trade-off between return and risk. The conditional variance measures "unexpected variation" or risk.

- A stylized fact for financial time series is a **non-constant volatility**:

> *"...large changes tend to be followed by large changes, of either sign, and small changes tend to be followed by small changes."*
> — Mandelbrot (1963)

  This is known as **volatility clustering**.

- ARCH and GARCH models attempt to model this feature. They specify equations for:
  1. Conditional mean
  2. Conditional variance

## 2. The ARCH Model Defined

### The ARCH Model

Consider the equation for $y_t$:

$$
y_t = x_t' \phi + \epsilon_t, \quad t = 1, 2, \dots, T. \tag{$*$}
$$

The predetermined regressor $x_t$ often includes lags of $y_t$ or dummies for special features.

This is an equation for the **conditional mean**, as

$$
E(y_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = x_t' \phi,
$$

with information set $\mathcal{I}_{t-1} = \{x_t, y_{t-1}, x_{t-1}, y_{t-2}, x_{t-2}, \ldots\}$.

In addition, the ARCH class of models specifies an equation for the **conditional variance**:

$$
V(y_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = E(\epsilon_t^2 \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) \equiv \sigma_t^2.
$$

### ARCH(1) Model

The ARCH(1) model is given by:

$$
y_t = x_t' \phi + \epsilon_t,
$$

$$
\epsilon_t = \sigma_t z_t,
$$

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2.
$$

To ensure that $\sigma_t^2 > 0$, we impose $\omega > 0$ and $\alpha \geq 0$, and we assume that

$$
E(z_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad E(z_t^2 \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = 1.
$$

It follows that:

$$
E(\epsilon_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = E(\sigma_t z_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = \sigma_t E(z_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = 0
$$

$$
E(\epsilon_t^2 \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = E((\sigma_t z_t)^2 \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = \sigma_t^2 E(z_t^2 \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = \sigma_t^2.
$$

To complete the model, we assume a distributional shape, e.g.

$$
z_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1} \stackrel{d}{=} N(0, 1) \quad \text{in which case} \quad y_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1} \stackrel{d}{=} N(x_t' \phi, \sigma_t^2).
$$

### Interpretation

The ARCH(1) model has:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2. \tag{$**$}
$$

If $\epsilon_{t-1}^2$ is high, the variance of the next shock, $\epsilon_t$, is large.

- This gives **volatility clustering**.
- Observe that positive and negative shocks, $\epsilon_t$, have the same effect.

### AR(1) Representation for Squared Innovations

It is useful to consider the decomposition:

$$
\epsilon_t^2 = E(\epsilon_t^2 \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) + v_t
$$

where $v_t = \epsilon_t^2 - \sigma_t^2$ is the (uncorrelated) surprise:

$$
E(v_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = 0.
$$

Now use $\sigma_t^2 = \epsilon_t^2 - v_t$ to rewrite $(**)$ as:

$$
\epsilon_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + v_t.
$$

The squared innovation $\epsilon_t^2$ follows an **AR(1) process**.

- Clear dependence in squared (and absolute) residuals.

### Unconditional Variance

The unconditional variance is given by:

$$
E(\epsilon_t^2) = \omega + \alpha E(\epsilon_{t-1}^2).
$$

If $0 \leq \alpha < 1$, the equation has solution $\sigma^2$ given by:

$$
\sigma^2 = \frac{\omega}{1 - \alpha}.
$$

The ARCH process is:
1. **Conditionally heteroskedastic.**
2. **Unconditionally homoskedastic.**

### Kurtosis and Heavy Tails

The probability mass in the tails of a distribution can be measured by the fourth moment.

For $z_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1} \stackrel{d}{=} N(0,1)$, the fourth moment of $\epsilon_t$ is given by:

$$
E(\epsilon_t^4) = \frac{3\omega^2(1 - \alpha^2)}{(1 - \alpha)^2(1 - 3\alpha^2)},
$$

which is finite for $1 - 3\alpha^2 > 0$, or $\alpha < \sqrt{1/3} \approx 0.577$.

The **kurtosis** is:

$$
K = \frac{E(\epsilon_t^4)}{E(\epsilon_t^2)^2} = 3\frac{(1 - \alpha^2)}{(1 - 3\alpha^2)}.
$$

Observe that for $\alpha > 0$ the distribution of $\epsilon_t$ has $K > 3$ and **fatter tails** than the Gaussian.

So the distribution of $\epsilon_t$ has heavy tails even if $z_t$ is Gaussian. Specifically, while the *conditional* distribution of $\epsilon_t$ is Gaussian, the *unconditional* distribution is non-Gaussian (heavy tailed).

### The ARCH(p) Model

Generalizes to an ARCH($p$) model:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha_1 \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \alpha_2 \epsilon_{t-2}^2 + \ldots + \alpha_p \epsilon_{t-p}^2.
$$

Properties:

1. We need $\omega > 0$ and $\alpha_i \geq 0$ for $i = 1, 2, \ldots, p$.
2. $\epsilon_t^2$ follows an AR($p$):

$$
\epsilon_t^2 = \omega + \alpha_1 \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \alpha_2 \epsilon_{t-2}^2 + \ldots + \alpha_p \epsilon_{t-p}^2 + v_t.
$$

3. If $\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \ldots + \alpha_p < 1$, then the process is stationary and weakly dependent, and the unconditional variance is:

$$
\sigma^2 = \frac{\omega}{1 - \alpha_1 - \ldots - \alpha_p}.
$$

## 3. OLS Estimation and a Test for No-ARCH

### OLS Estimation and Misspecification Testing

Consider the linear regression model (neglecting ARCH effects):

$$
y_t = x_t' \phi + \epsilon_t
$$

The OLS estimator $\hat{\phi}$ is consistent under the usual assumptions:

1. $E(\epsilon_t \mid x_t) = 0$
2. A LLN applies

- You should use **heteroskedasticity-consistent standard errors**.
- The OLS estimator for $\phi$ is **inefficient**. There exists a non-linear estimator that takes the ARCH structure into account.

### Motivation: Estimating Mean Return

Suppose that financial returns $\{y_t\}_{t=1}^T$ are generated by:

$$
y_t = \mu + \varepsilon_t, \quad \varepsilon_t = \sigma_t z_t, \quad z_t \text{ iid } N(0,1), \quad \sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \varepsilon_{t-1}^2.
$$

A well-known estimator of the mean return $\mu$ is the average:

$$
\hat{\mu}_{\text{AVG}} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T y_t
$$

and $\hat{\mu}_{\text{AVG}} = \hat{\mu}_{\text{OLS}}$ since $\hat{\mu}_{\text{AVG}} = \arg\min_{\mu \in \mathbb{R}} \sum_{t=1}^T (y_t - \mu)^2$.

If $\alpha_0 < 1$, then by a LLN and CLT (as $T \to \infty$):

- **Consistency:** $\hat{\mu}_{\text{OLS}} \xrightarrow{p} \mu_0$
- **Asymptotic normality:** $\sqrt{T}(\hat{\mu}_{\text{OLS}} - \mu_0) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, V_{\text{OLS}})$

with $V_{\text{OLS}} = E[\varepsilon_t^2] = \omega_0(1 - \alpha_0)^{-1}$.

### Taking ARCH-Effects into Account

Since $y_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1} \sim N(\mu, \sigma_t^2)$, an alternative is the MLE $\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}}$.

Under the same assumptions, $\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} \xrightarrow{p} \mu_0$ and

$$
\sqrt{T}(\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} - \mu_0) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, V_{\text{MLE}}).
$$

The MLE is asymptotically efficient and:

$$
V_{\text{MLE}} < V_{\text{OLS}}.
$$

*(Figure shows Monte Carlo comparison with $\mu_0 = 5$, $\omega_0 = 1$, $\alpha_0 = 0.9$, $T = 2000$, $10^4$ replications. The OLS distribution is much more spread out than the MLE distribution.)*

### Misspecification Test for No-ARCH

Use the **Breusch-Pagan LM test** for heteroskedasticity. The auxiliary regression model is:

$$
\hat{\epsilon}_t^2 = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \hat{\epsilon}_{t-1}^2 + \beta_2 \hat{\epsilon}_{t-2}^2 + \ldots + \beta_p \hat{\epsilon}_{t-p}^2 + \text{error}_t,
$$

where the residuals $\{\hat{\epsilon}_t\}_{t=1}^T$ are estimated from the linear regression. Under the null of no ARCH:

$$
H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \ldots = \beta_p = 0.
$$

The test statistic is:

$$
\zeta_{\text{ARCH}} = T \cdot R^2 \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2(p) \quad \text{under } H_0.
$$

> **Note:** The ARCH test also has power against residual autocorrelation. Always test for no-autocorrelation first.

### Example: S&P 500 Returns

A model for the conditional mean:

$$
y_t = \underset{(0.0168)}{0.0275} - \underset{(0.0234)}{0.0715}\, y_{t-1} - \underset{(0.0294)}{0.0528}\, y_{t-2} + \hat{\epsilon}_t,
$$

with standard errors in parentheses. Here $R^2 = 0.00739$.

The auxiliary regression for squared residuals:

$$
\hat{\epsilon}_t^2 = \underset{(0.0613)}{0.462} + \underset{(0.0134)}{0.0513}\, \hat{\epsilon}_{t-1}^2 + \underset{(0.0133)}{0.268}\, \hat{\epsilon}_{t-2}^2 + \ldots + \underset{(0.0134)}{0.212}\, \hat{\epsilon}_{t-5}^2 + \text{error}.
$$

Many significant and $R^2 = 0.219$. The LM test statistic is:

$$
\chi_{\text{ARCH}} = T R^2 = 5317 \times 0.219 = 1165.5,
$$

which is highly significant in the asymptotic $\chi^2(5)$ distribution.

**We reject the null of no ARCH.**

## 4. Estimation of ARCH Models

### Maximum Likelihood Estimation of the ARCH Model

Consider the simple ARCH(1) case:

$$
y_t = \mu + \epsilon_t, \quad \mu \in \mathbb{R}
$$

$$
\epsilon_t = \sigma_t z_t,
$$

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2.
$$

Assume conditional normality:

$$
z_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1} \stackrel{d}{=} N(0,1).
$$

With $\theta = (\mu, \omega, \alpha)'$, the Gaussian likelihood contribution is:

$$
\ell(\theta \mid y_t, \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\, \sigma_t^2(\theta)}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2} \frac{\epsilon_t^2(\theta)}{\sigma_t^2(\theta)}\right)
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\, (\omega + \alpha(y_{t-1} - \mu)^2)}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2} \frac{(y_t - \mu)^2}{\omega + \alpha(y_{t-1} - \mu)^2}\right)
$$

Maximize with respect to $\theta = (\mu, \omega, \alpha)'$.

> **No analytical solution for MLE.** Use numerical optimization methods.

### Properties of the (Quasi-)MLE

Consider a general class of conditional volatility models (including GARCH):

$$
y_t = \mu_t + \epsilon_t, \quad \epsilon_t = \sigma_t z_t,
$$

with $\mu_t$ and $\sigma_t^2$ functions of a parameter vector $\theta$.

Assume that $(z_t)$ is an i.i.d. process with $E[z_t] = 0$ and $E[z_t^2] = 1$.

Suppose we estimate $\theta$ using the Gaussian log-likelihood:

$$
\sum_{t=1}^T \log \ell_t(\theta) = \sum_{t=1}^T \log\left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\,\sigma_t^2(\theta)}} \exp\left(-\frac{(y_t - \mu_t(\theta))^2}{2\sigma_t^2(\theta)}\right)\right].
$$

> If $z_t$ is non-Gaussian, this is the **quasi-log-likelihood** function.

Under suitable conditions, $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max \sum_{t=1}^T \log \ell_t(\theta)$ satisfies:

- **Consistency:** $\hat{\theta} \xrightarrow{p} \theta_0$
- **Asymptotic normality:** $\sqrt{T}(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \mathcal{I}^{-1}\mathcal{J}\mathcal{I}^{-1})$

with

$$
\mathcal{I} = -E\left[\frac{\partial^2 \log \ell_t(\theta_0)}{\partial\theta\,\partial\theta'}\right], \quad \mathcal{J} = E\left[\frac{\partial \log \ell_t(\theta_0)}{\partial\theta}\frac{\partial \log \ell_t(\theta_0)}{\partial\theta'}\right].
$$

- The QMLE $\hat{\theta}$ has the usual **sandwich asymptotic covariance matrix**.
- If $z_t$ is Gaussian, then $\hat{\theta}$ is the MLE, $\mathcal{I} = \mathcal{J}$, and:

$$
\sqrt{T}(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \mathcal{I}^{-1}). \tag{$***$}
$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Properties when $\mu_t \equiv 0$</strong></summary>

In the case where $\mu_t \equiv 0$, such that $y_t = \epsilon_t = \sigma_t z_t$, it holds (under suitable conditions):

$$
\sqrt{T}(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} N\left(0, \frac{\kappa}{2}\mathcal{I}^{-1}\right),
$$

with

$$
\mathcal{I} = \frac{1}{2} E\left[\frac{1}{\sigma_t^4(\theta_0)} \frac{\partial \sigma_t^2(\theta_0)}{\partial\theta} \frac{\partial \sigma_t^2(\theta_0)}{\partial\theta'}\right] \quad \text{and} \quad \kappa = E[z_t^4] - 1 < \infty.
$$

- If $z_t$ is Gaussian, $E[z_t^4] = 3$, so $\kappa/2 = 1$, recovering $(***)$.
- The asymptotic covariance is "increasing" in $\kappa$: the heavier the tails of $z_t$, the more estimation uncertainty.

</details>

### Alternative Distributions

Other (typically fat-tailed) distributions for $z_t$ can also be used.

An example is the **scaled Student's $t(\nu)$-distribution**, with likelihood contribution:

$$
\ell_t(\theta) = \frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{\nu+1}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{\nu}{2}\right)\sqrt{\pi(\nu-2)\sigma_t^2(\theta)}} \left(1 + \frac{\epsilon_t^2(\theta)}{(\nu-2)\sigma_t^2(\theta)}\right)^{-\frac{\nu+1}{2}},
$$

where $\Gamma(\cdot)$ is the Gamma function.

- The distribution is scaled: $E[z_t] = 0$ and $E[z_t^2] = 1$.
- Typically, the degree of freedom parameter $\nu$ is also estimated. It determines how fat the tails are.

### Example: S&P 500 Returns

Sample period: January 6, 1997 – February 27, 2018.

Using a Gaussian likelihood, the AR(2)-ARCH(3) model:

$$
y_t = \underset{(0.0140)}{0.06492} - \underset{(0.0208)}{0.03717}\, y_{t-1} - \underset{(0.0345)}{0.03890}\, y_{t-2} + \epsilon_t
$$

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \underset{(0.0316)}{0.4918} + \underset{(0.0263)}{0.1486}\, \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \underset{(0.0436)}{0.3411}\, \epsilon_{t-2}^2 + \underset{(0.0286)}{0.2178}\, \epsilon_{t-3}^2.
$$

Observations from estimating models with different $p$:

1. Many lags are significant.
2. Difficult to precisely pin down the shape of the memory structure.
3. Estimated coefficients are often relatively unstable between models.

## 5. The GARCH Model Defined

### GARCH Defined

The popular **GARCH(1,1)** model is defined by:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2, \quad \text{with } \sigma_0^2 > 0 \text{ given}.
$$

For $\sigma_t^2$ to be non-negative we require $\omega > 0$, $\alpha \geq 0$, and $\beta \geq 0$.

### ARMA Representation

Using the definition $\sigma_t^2 = \epsilon_t^2 - v_t$:

$$
\epsilon_t^2 = \omega + (\alpha + \beta)\epsilon_{t-1}^2 + v_t - \beta v_{t-1},
$$

which is an **ARMA(1,1) model** for the squared innovation.

**Stationarity and weak dependence** require that $\alpha + \beta < 1$.

### GARCH as Infinite ARCH

Using recursive substitution:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\sigma_{t-1}^2
$$

$$
= \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta(\omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-2}^2 + \beta\sigma_{t-2}^2)
$$

$$
= \omega(1 + \beta) + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \alpha\beta \epsilon_{t-2}^2 + \beta^2 \sigma_{t-2}^2
$$

$$
\vdots
$$

$$
= \frac{\omega}{1 - \beta} + \alpha \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \beta^{j-1} \epsilon_{t-j}^2.
$$

**The GARCH model is a restricted infinite ARCH model.**

### GARCH(p,q) Model

Generalizes to:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \alpha_j \epsilon_{t-j}^2 + \sum_{j=1}^{q} \beta_j \sigma_{t-j}^2,
$$

with stationarity condition $\sum_{j=1}^{p} \alpha_j + \sum_{j=1}^{q} \beta_j < 1$.

### Forecasting

An important application is **prediction of future volatility**:

$$
\sigma_{T+h|T}^2 = E(\epsilon_{T+h}^2 \mid \mathcal{I}_T).
$$

For the GARCH(1,1) model:

$$
\sigma_{T+1|T}^2 = \sigma^2 + \alpha(\epsilon_T^2 - \sigma^2) + \beta(\sigma_T^2 - \sigma^2),
$$

where $\epsilon_T$ is in the information set.

For longer horizons:

$$
\sigma_{T+h|T}^2 = \sigma^2 + (\alpha + \beta)\left(\sigma_{T+h-1|T}^2 - \sigma^2\right),
$$

which is an **exponential convergence** with speed $\alpha + \beta$.

### Example: S&P 500 Returns

Sample period: January 6, 1997 – February 27, 2018.

Gaussian AR(2)-GARCH(1,1) model:

$$
y_t = \underset{(0.0117)}{0.06133} - \underset{(0.0143)}{0.04659}\, y_{t-1} - \underset{(0.0159)}{0.01829}\, y_{t-2} + \epsilon_t
$$

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \underset{(0.00448)}{0.01753} + \underset{(0.0149)}{0.1041}\, \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \underset{(0.0151)}{0.8850}\, \sigma_{t-1}^2.
$$

Key observations:

1. The lagged variance is large and significant.
2. $\hat{\alpha} + \hat{\beta} = 0.989 \approx 1$, which is often the case empirically.
3. The news impact curve ($\sigma_t^2$ as function of $\epsilon_{t-1}$) is **symmetric**. Positive and negative shocks have the same effect.

*(Figures show: (A) Conditional standard deviation, (B) Conditional standard deviation vs. VIX, (C) Conditional variance forecast, (D) News impact curves comparing basic GARCH, threshold model, and asymmetric model.)*

### The IGARCH Phenomenon

Quite often, $\hat{\alpha} + \hat{\beta} \approx 1$. The model with $\alpha + \beta = 1$ is known as the **IGARCH model**. The test for IGARCH is $\chi^2$.

- IGARCH is often interpreted as a sign of **misspecification**. Could be implied by level shifts in the unconditional variance.
- A consequence of $\hat{\alpha} + \hat{\beta} \approx 1$ is that the unconditional variance is poorly estimated:

$$
\sigma^2 = \frac{\omega}{1 - \alpha - \beta}.
$$

### Variance Targeting

If the unconditional variance is poorly estimated, forecasts are problematic.

We use $\omega = \sigma^2(1 - \alpha - \beta)$ to rewrite the model as:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \sigma^2(1 - \alpha - \beta) + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2.
$$

Instead of estimating $(\sigma^2, \alpha, \beta)$ or $(\omega, \alpha, \beta)$, we can fix $\sigma^2$ to some robust estimate (e.g., the empirical variance $\hat{s}^2$) and only estimate $(\alpha, \beta)$.

This is known as **variance targeting**.

## 6. Extensions of the GARCH Model

### Threshold Asymmetric Model

Sometimes negative shocks have a different impact. To model this:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \kappa \epsilon_{t-1}^2 \mathbb{I}(\epsilon_{t-1} < 0) + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2,
$$

where $\mathbb{I}(\epsilon_{t-1} < 0) = 1$ if $\epsilon_{t-1} < 0$ and zero otherwise, and $\kappa \geq 0$.

Equivalently:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \begin{cases} \omega + (\alpha + \kappa)\epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\sigma_{t-1}^2 & \text{if } \epsilon_{t-1} < 0 \\ \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\sigma_{t-1}^2 & \text{if } \epsilon_{t-1} \geq 0 \end{cases}
$$

If $z_t$ has a symmetric distribution, the **stationarity condition** is:

$$
\alpha + \beta + \frac{1}{2}\kappa < 1.
$$

#### Example: S&P 500 Returns (Threshold Model)

Estimated with $z_t \stackrel{d}{=} t(\nu)$:

$$
y_t = \underset{(0.0109)}{0.05310} - \underset{(0.0129)}{0.04685}\, y_{t-1} - \underset{(0.0142)}{0.02498}\, y_{t-2} + \epsilon_t
$$

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \underset{(0.0031)}{0.01263} + \underset{(\ldots)}{0}\, \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \underset{(0.0214)}{0.1631}\, \epsilon_{t-1}^2 \mathbb{I}(\epsilon_{t-1} < 0) + \underset{(0.0114)}{0.9073}\, \sigma_{t-1}^2,
$$

with $\hat{\nu} = 7.21$. Log-likelihood: $-7241.557$.

- **Pronounced asymmetry.** Only negative shocks affect the conditional variance.

### Asymmetric Model

An alternative form of asymmetry:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha(\epsilon_{t-1} - \gamma)^2 + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2,
$$

where zero is no longer the neutral shock.

#### Example: S&P 500 Returns (Asymmetric Model)

$$
y_t = \underset{(0.0103)}{0.04607} - \underset{(0.0128)}{0.05290}\, y_{t-1} - \underset{(0.0140)}{0.03223}\, y_{t-2} + \epsilon_t
$$

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \underset{(\ldots)}{0} + \underset{(0.000674)}{0.1034}\, (\epsilon_{t-1} - 0.4974)^2 + \underset{(0.0113)}{0.8763}\, \sigma_{t-1}^2
$$

with $\hat{\nu} = 7.420$. Log-likelihood: $-7253.190$.

- Again support for asymmetry. Lower likelihood than threshold model.

### GARCH in Mean

The volatility $\sigma_t^2$ affects the conditional mean — interpreted as a **risk premium**:

$$
y_t = x_t'\phi + \delta \sigma_t^2 + \epsilon_t
$$

$$
y_t = x_t'\phi + \delta \sigma_t + \epsilon_t
$$

$$
y_t = x_t'\phi + \delta \log(\sigma_t^2) + \epsilon_t.
$$

#### Example: S&P 500 Returns (GARCH-in-Mean)

$$

### Explanatory Variables and EGARCH

**GARCH-X** models with (exogenous) covariates in the conditional variance equation:

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2 + \xi d_t,
$$

with $\xi \geq 0$ and $d_t$ some non-negative predetermined variable.

**Exponential GARCH (EGARCH):**

$$
\sigma_t^2 = \exp\left(\omega + \alpha z_{t-1} + \gamma(|z_{t-1}| - E(|z_{t-1}|)) + \beta \log \sigma_{t-1}^2\right).
$$

Appealing features:

1. **No restrictions on the parameters**, as $\sigma_t^2 = \exp(\cdot) > 0$. Convenient in cases with exogenous variables.
2. **Variance driven by scaled residuals** $z_{t-1} = \epsilon_{t-1}/\sigma_{t-1}$, not $\epsilon_{t-1}$. Large shocks have smaller effects if variance is already high. News impact curve ($\sigma_t^2$ as a function of $\epsilon_{t-1}$) is time varying.

## 7. Multivariate GARCH

### Multivariate GARCH Models

ARCH models can be extended to multivariate cases. As an example, let:

$$
Y_t = \begin{pmatrix} y_{1,t} \\ y_{2,t} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \Delta\log(\text{Apple}_t) \\ \Delta\log(\text{Nasdaq}_t) \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Time-varying (conditional) variances and covariance are of interest.

Consider a portfolio $\omega y_{1,t} + (1 - \omega)y_{2,t}$. The risk of this portfolio is:

$$
V_{t-1}\left(\omega y_{1,t} + (1 - \omega)y_{2,t}\right) = \omega^2 V_{t-1}(y_{1,t}) + (1 - \omega)^2 V_{t-1}(y_{2,t}) + 2\omega(1 - \omega)\operatorname{cov}_{t-1}(y_{1,t}, y_{2,t}).
$$

### Model Specification

A simple multivariate ARCH model:

$$
Y_t = \mu + \epsilon_t, \quad \epsilon_t = \Omega_t^{1/2} Z_t, \quad Z_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1} \stackrel{d}{=} N(0, I_2),
$$

where

$$
\Omega_t = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{1,t}^2 & \sigma_{12,t} \\ \sigma_{21,t} & \sigma_{2,t}^2 \end{pmatrix}
$$

describes the time-varying conditional variances and covariance.

### Requirements and the CCC Model

The requirement is that $\Omega_t$ is **symmetric and positive definite**:

$$
\sigma_{12,t} = \sigma_{21,t}, \quad \sigma_{1,t}^2 > 0, \quad \sigma_{2,t}^2 > 0, \quad \sigma_{1,t}^2 \sigma_{2,t}^2 - \sigma_{12,t}^2 > 0.
$$

A simple example is the **Constant Conditional Correlation (CCC) model**:

$$
\Omega_t = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{1,t} & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_{2,t} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho \\ \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{1,t} & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_{2,t} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{1,t}^2 & \rho\,\sigma_{1,t}\,\sigma_{2,t} \\ \rho\,\sigma_{1,t}\,\sigma_{2,t} & \sigma_{2,t}^2 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Properties:

1. The conditional correlation is **constant**, $\rho$.
2. The conditional variances are chosen as **univariate GARCH**:

$$
\sigma_{1,t}^2 = \omega_1 + \alpha_1 \epsilon_{1,t-1}^2 + \beta_1 \sigma_{1,t-1}^2
$$

$$
\sigma_{2,t}^2 = \omega_2 + \alpha_2 \epsilon_{2,t-1}^2 + \beta_2 \sigma_{2,t-1}^2.
$$

### Optimal Portfolio Choice

Recall the portfolio $\omega\,\text{Apple}_t + (1 - \omega)\,\text{Nasdaq}_t$ with variance:

$$
P_t = \omega^2 \sigma_{1,t}^2 + (1 - \omega)^2 \sigma_{2,t}^2 + 2\omega(1 - \omega)\sigma_{12,t}.
$$

The FOC for minimizing the portfolio variance:

$$
\frac{\partial P_t}{\partial \omega} = 2\omega\,\sigma_{1,t}^2 - 2(1 - \omega)\,\sigma_{2,t}^2 + 2\sigma_{12,t} - 4\omega\,\sigma_{12,t} = 0.
$$

The weight to Apple in the **minimum-variance portfolio** is:

$$
\omega_t = \frac{\sigma_{2,t}^2 - \sigma_{12,t}}{\sigma_{1,t}^2 + \sigma_{2,t}^2 - 2\sigma_{12,t}}.
$$

## 8. Applications of GARCH

- **Efficiency gains** (MLE).
- **Modelling within risk management.**
- **Value-at-Risk:**

$$
P(y_t < \text{VaR}_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = 1\%.
$$

- **Expected Shortfall:**

$$
E[y_t \mid y_t < \text{VaR}_t,\, \mathcal{I}_{t-1}].
$$

- **Derivative pricing.** E.g., pricing of equity options under time-varying volatility.
- **Dynamic portfolio allocation** (multivariate GARCH).

## 9. Extra: Mean-Variance Investor

### Mean-Variance Optimization

Consider an investor who can invest in a **risky asset** and a **risk-free asset** with returns $R_{t+1}$ and $r_{f,t+1}$, respectively, from time $t$ to $t+1$.

A portfolio consisting of these two assets has return (approximately):

$$
R_{t+1}^{(p)} = w_t R_{t+1} + (1 - w_t) r_{f,t+1},
$$

where $w_t$ is the weight of the risky asset. The weight is chosen at time $t$, so $w_t \in \mathcal{I}_t$.

Let $r_{t+1} = R_{t+1} - r_{f,t+1}$ denote the **excess return**, and note that:

$$
R_{t+1}^{(p)} = w_t r_{t+1} + r_{f,t+1}.
$$

### Utility Specification

The investor has expected utility:

$$
U_t = E\left[R_{t+1}^{(p)} \mid \mathcal{I}_t\right] - \frac{\gamma}{2} V\left[R_{t+1}^{(p)} \mid \mathcal{I}_t\right],
$$

where $\gamma > 0$ is a **risk aversion parameter**.

Assuming the risk-free return is known a priori ($r_{f,t+1} \in \mathcal{I}_t$):

$$
E\left[R_{t+1}^{(p)} \mid \mathcal{I}_t\right] = w_t E[r_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t] + r_{f,t+1},
$$

$$
V\left[R_{t+1}^{(p)} \mid \mathcal{I}_t\right] = w_t^2 V[r_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t].
$$

### Optimal Weight

Maximizing expected utility with respect to $w_t$ gives the F.O.C:

$$
\frac{\partial U_t}{\partial w_t} = E[r_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t] - \gamma w_t V[r_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t] = 0,
$$

with solution:

$$
w_t^* = \frac{1}{\gamma} \left(\frac{E[r_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t]}{V[r_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t]}\right) = \frac{1}{\gamma} \left(\frac{E[R_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t] - r_{f,t+1}}{V[R_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t]}\right).
$$
