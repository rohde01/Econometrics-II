\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This paper estimates a univariate model for U.S.\ quarterly GDP growth to produce one-step-ahead forecasts and 10\% Growth-at-Risk (GaR) estimates.
We estimate AR(1)--AR(4) and ARMA(1,1) models on 1990Q1--2009Q4 and evaluate 60 out-of-sample observations from 2010Q1--2024Q4.
\newline
\newline
Model selection using BIC and residual diagnostics favors AR(2).
Its forecasts are not statistically different from a naive historical-mean benchmark (Diebold-Mariano $p=0.24$, HAC standard error), and neither AR(2)-based nor unconditional GaR is rejected by the 10\% coverage test.
Overall, the results support recent evidence that simple univariate models can remain competitive for forecasting and risk measurement (Adrian et al., 2025).

\section{Description of the Data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The data consist of quarterly observations from 1990Q1 to 2024Q4. The variable of interest is $y_t = \log(\text{GDPC1}_t) - \log(\text{GDPC1}_{t-4})$, the log year-on-year growth rate of U.S.\ Real GDP (GDPC1), retrieved from the FRED database; the year-on-year transformation removes seasonal effects without seasonal adjustment.
\newline
Figure~\ref{fig:gdp_growth} plots the full sample. The series fluctuates around a positive mean with no apparent trend, consistent with stationarity. Two episodes stand out: the Global Financial Crisis (2008--2009) and the COVID-19 trough in 2020Q2 (roughly $-9\%$, followed by a sharp V-shaped recovery), motivating the use of a low-order autoregressive model.
\newline
Figure~\ref{fig:acf_pacf} shows the sample autocorrelation function (ACF) and partial autocorrelation function (PACF) for the training period 1990Q1--2009Q4. The ACF decays gradually over several lags, indicating persistence, while the PACF drops sharply after lag 2 with remaining partial autocorrelations close to zero. This pattern is the classic signature of an AR(2) process, and guides our subsequent model selection.

\section{Econometric Theory}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────



We consider the ARMA($p$,$q$) model
\begin{equation}\label{eq:arma}
  y_t = \delta + \phi_1 y_{t-1} + \cdots + \phi_p y_{t-p}
        + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \cdots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q},
  \quad \varepsilon_t \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} N(0,\sigma^2),
\end{equation}
where $y_t$ is the log year-on-year GDP growth rate and $\delta$ is the intercept. A pure AR($p$) corresponds to $q=0$. Stationarity requires that all roots of the AR polynomial $1 - \phi_1 L - \cdots - \phi_p L^p = 0$ lie outside the unit circle, under which the unconditional mean equals:
$$\mu = \delta/(1 - \phi_1 - \cdots - \phi_p)$$.

\subsection{Estimation}

For AR($p$) models, parameters are estimated by OLS.
With Gaussian errors, this is numerically equivalent to maximum likelihood.
Under standard assumptions (stationarity, weak dependence, and zero conditional mean errors), the estimator is consistent and asymptotically normal.
\newline
\newline
For ARMA($p$,$q$) models with $q\geq 1$, OLS is not feasible because lagged error terms are unobserved.
We therefore estimate these models by Gaussian maximum likelihood, where residuals are computed recursively.
Under $\varepsilon_t \sim N(0,\sigma^2)$, the (conditional) Gaussian log-likelihood is
$$
\ell(\boldsymbol{\phi},\boldsymbol{\theta},\sigma^2)
=
-\frac{T}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)
-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\varepsilon_t^2,
$$
which is maximized with respect to $\boldsymbol{\phi}$, $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, and $\sigma^2$.
\newline
Model selection follows a general-to-specific (GETS) strategy.
We begin with AR(4) and test down to simpler nested models using likelihood-ratio tests.
Because LR tests are sensitive to non-normality, we confirm each reduction with AIC and BIC, and use BIC as the main criterion because it penalizes model complexity more strongly.


\subsection{Forecasting}

The one-step-ahead forecast from model~(\ref{eq:arma}) is the conditional mean
\[
  \hat{y}_{t+1} = E[y_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{I}_t]
  = \delta + \phi_1 y_t + \cdots + \phi_p y_{t-p+1}
    + \theta_1 \varepsilon_t + \cdots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q+1}.
\]
Parameters are fixed at their training-sample estimates. Residuals $\hat{\varepsilon}_t$ required for the MA terms are updated recursively via $\hat{\varepsilon}_t = y_t - \hat{y}_t$ for $t > T$.
\newline
Forecast accuracy is assessed against the naive benchmark $\bar{y} = T^{-1}\sum_{t=1}^{T} y_t$ using root mean squared error (RMSE). Statistical significance is evaluated by the Diebold-Mariano (DM) test: define $d_t = e_{\mathrm{model},t}^2 - e_{\mathrm{naive},t}^2$ where $e_{j,t} = y_t - \hat{y}_{j,t}$; then under $H_0\colon E[d_t]=0$,
\[
  t_{\mathrm{DM}} = \frac{\bar{d}}{\widehat{\mathrm{s.e.}}(\bar{d})} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1).
\]
Since $d_t$ is typically autocorrelated, $\widehat{\mathrm{s.e.}}(\bar{d})$ uses a HAC variance estimator, implemented by regressing $d_t$ on a constant.

\subsection{Growth-at-Risk}

The one-period Growth-at-Risk at risk level $\alpha\in(0,1)$, denoted $\mathrm{GaR}^\alpha_t$, is the $\alpha$-quantile of the conditional distribution of $y_t$ given $\mathcal{I}_{t-1}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gar}
  P(y_t \leq \mathrm{GaR}^\alpha_t \mid \mathcal{I}_{t-1}) = \alpha,\quad \varepsilon_t \sim N(0,\sigma^2)\Rightarrow \mathrm{GaR}^\alpha_t = \hat{y}_t + \sigma\Phi^{-1}(\alpha).
\end{equation}
where $\Phi^{-1}(\alpha)$ is the $\alpha$-quantile of the standard normal.
\newline
\newline
Evaluation uses the hit indicator $\mathrm{hit}_t = \mathbf{1}\{y_t < \mathrm{GaR}^\alpha_t\}$ and the violation rate $\hat{p} = T^{-1}\sum_t \mathrm{hit}_t$. Under correct specification $(\mathrm{hit}_t)$ is i.i.d.\ Bernoulli($\alpha$) and we test $H_0\colon E[\mathrm{hit}_t]=\alpha$ using the $t$-statistic $t = (\hat{p}-\alpha)/\sqrt{\alpha(1-\alpha)/T}$, which is asymptotically $N(0,1)$. Additional discriminating power is provided by the tick loss
\[
  \mathcal{T}_\alpha(y_t, \mathrm{GaR}^\alpha_t) = (\alpha - \mathrm{hit}_t)(y_t - \mathrm{GaR}^\alpha_t).
\]

\section{Empirical Analysis}

\subsection{Model Selection}

Figure~\ref{fig:acf_pacf} motivated a focus on AR models: the PACF is statistically significant at lags 1 and 2 and negligible thereafter. Following the GETS principle, we estimate AR(1)--AR(4) and ARMA(1,1) and compare them in Table~\ref{tab:model_selection}, then apply sequential LR tests starting from AR(4) to justify reducing the model.
\newline
\newline
Testing AR(4) vs.\ AR(3) ($H_0\colon \phi_4=0$) gives $\xi_{\mathrm{LR}}=0.08$ ($p=0.78$): fail to reject, reduce. Testing AR(3) vs.\ AR(2) ($H_0\colon \phi_3=0$) gives $\xi_{\mathrm{LR}}=2.29$ ($p=0.13$): again fail to reject, yielding AR(2) as the GETS outcome. Information criteria confirm: AR(2) achieves the best BIC ($-542.33$), while AR(1) is excluded due to significant residual autocorrelation (Ljung-Box lag-8 $p=0.00$). We therefore select \textbf{AR(2)} as the preferred specification.

\subsection{Misspecification Testing}

Three misspecification tests are applied to the AR(2) training-sample residuals.
\newline
The Ljung-Box test (adjusted for $p=2$ AR parameters, $\sim\chi^2(h-p)$) yields $Q(4)=8.67$ ($p=0.013$) and $Q(8)=12.25$ ($p=0.057$). The borderline rejections are attributable to the 2008Q4 GFC outlier inflating nearby autocorrelations, not to a failure of the AR(2) lag structure.
\newline
\newline
The Jarque-Bera test yields $\mathrm{JB}=8.63$ ($p=0.013$), rejecting normality at the 5\% level due to excess kurtosis of $1.31$ from GFC residuals in 2008Q4--2009Q1. Adding intervention dummies for these quarters reduces the statistic to $\mathrm{JB}=0.26$ ($p=0.879$), confirming the rejection is outlier-driven. We retain the plain AR(2) for forecasting, but the result means the estimator is formally a quasi-MLE (QMLE): $t$-statistics remain valid (sandwich covariance $\Omega^* = \mathcal{I}^{-1}\mathcal{J}\mathcal{I}^{-1}$), though the Gaussian GaR formula~(\ref{eq:gar}) may mis-calibrate the lower tail.
\newline
The ARCH-LM test at four lags yields $7.98$ ($p=0.092$), failing to reject conditional homoskedasticity at the 5\% level.

\subsection{Preferred Model: Estimates and Interpretation}

Table~\ref{tab:estimates} reports the ML estimates for the AR(2) model over 1990Q1--2009Q4. The estimated unconditional mean is $\hat{\mu} = 0.0262$ (standard error $0.006$), corresponding to an average annual GDP growth of approximately 2.6\%, which is in line with the U.S.\ long-run average. Both AR coefficients are highly significant: $\hat{\phi}_1 = 1.359$ ($t=11.0$) and $\hat{\phi}_2 = -0.512$ ($t=-4.1$). The sum $\hat{\phi}_1 + \hat{\phi}_2 = 0.847 < 1$ confirms stationarity; the roots are complex conjugates (modulus $\approx 0.72$), implying damped oscillations around the mean. The residual standard deviation $\hat{\sigma}=0.010$ enters the GaR formula as the scale of the conditional distribution.

\subsection{Growth-at-Risk Evaluation}

Figure~\ref{fig:gar} and Table~\ref{tab:gar_eval} present the GaR evaluation.
\newline
The AR(2) GaR has a violation rate of $\hat{p}=0.150$; the coverage $t$-test ($t=1.291$, $p=0.197$) cannot reject correct coverage. The $\mathrm{GaR}^{\mathrm{np}}$ series shows the mirror pattern ($\hat{p}=0.050$, same $|t|$, $p=0.197$), also not rejected. The AR(2) excess violations are largely attributable to the two COVID-19 quarters of 2020. On tick loss, $\mathrm{GaR}^{\mathrm{np}}$ achieves a marginally lower mean loss ($0.0040$ vs.\ $0.0046$). Overall, the AR(2) GaR provides useful time-variation relative to the constant benchmark but does not achieve demonstrably better calibration over this evaluation window.

\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Three limitations deserve mention.
First, the GaR formula~(\ref{eq:gar}) is derived under Gaussian errors, while the JB rejection indicates non-Gaussian residual behavior driven by crisis observations.
This matters most for tail risk: Gaussian GaR can be too optimistic or too conservative in the lower tail when kurtosis rises.
Hence, the non-rejection of the 10\% coverage test should be read as weak evidence of calibration, not strong validation of the full conditional distribution.
\newline
Second, the empirical design is intentionally parsimonious but restrictive.
Parameters are estimated once on 1990Q1--2009Q4 and then held fixed, which rules out coefficient drift, level shifts, and changing shock volatility during the evaluation period.
In addition, the univariate specification ignores macro-financial state variables that may contain incremental information about downside risk, so the model can be well-calibrated on average while still missing variation in the conditional tail over time.
\newline
Third, the evaluation window is short and heavily influenced by rare extremes.
The COVID-19 collapse and rebound account for a disproportionate share of squared forecast errors and GaR exceedances, so headline metrics (RMSE differences, violation rates, and tick loss) are sensitive to a few quarters.
This concentration makes model ranking less stable and limits external validity: conclusions are most credible as evidence of robustness in an unusually turbulent sample, not as a universal statement about performance across regimes.

\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

An AR(2) model for U.S.\ quarterly GDP growth was selected by GETS and confirmed by BIC. Misspecification tests pass at the 5\% level, with the Jarque-Bera rejection outlier-driven and $t$-statistics remaining valid under QMLE. Out-of-sample, forecasts are statistically indistinguishable from the naive benchmark (DM $p=0.24$), and both GaR series pass the 10\% coverage test. The dominant driver of deviations in both exercises is the COVID-19 shock, consistent with the broader finding that simple univariate models remain competitive in macroeconomic forecasting and risk quantification.
