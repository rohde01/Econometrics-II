## Writing and Structure: Assessment Criteria

> The assignment must be written in a formal and precise language with an academic structure.

> The assignment must be readable on its own. In particular, it should be clear which question the assignment addresses, how it is done, and what the conclusion is.

---

## Writing Your Assignment

**Crucial:**

- Your assignment must be readable on its own.
- Write the assignment as if the reader has no prior knowledge of the assignment, but general knowledge about econometrics. *(Write it for a student who followed this course in the past.)*
- Explain what you have done and why you have done it, so that your empirical analysis is reproducible.

**Additionally:**

- Write in a formal academic language. Get to the point!
- Think about the structure of your assignment.
- Be precise and write in a compact way.
- Be consistent with the notation and avoid typos.
- Present the theory and your results in a logical order.

## What is a Good Structure?

**Option A:**
1. Introduction
2. Econometric theory
3. Description of the data
4. Empirical analysis
5. Discussion
6. Conclusion

**Or Option B:**
1. Introduction
2. Description of the data
3. Econometric theory
4. Empirical analysis
5. Discussion
6. Conclusion

## Introduction

**What to include:**

1. **Main question** — What is the question you seek to answer? Just one or two main questions.
2. **Motivation** — Why is the main question of interest?
3. **Econometric model/method** — Which econometric model(s) do you use to answer the question?
4. **Conclusion** — What is the conclusion to your main question? Are the results in line with your expectations/economic theory?

---

## The Econometric Theory: Assessment Criteria

> The section on econometric theory should include the following:

1. A precise definition and interpretation of the model considered and its properties.
2. A precise description of the estimator used. In particular, a precise account for the assumptions used to derive the estimator should be given.
3. A precise presentation of the necessary assumptions for consistent estimation and valid inference. This includes a precise presentation of the null hypotheses, test statistics, and asymptotic distributions used to test relevant hypotheses.
4. The theory must be presented in a logical order with a correct and consistent use of notation.

---

## The Econometric Theory

**What to include:**

- **The model** — Present the econometric model(s) you use. Start with the equation, then describe the assumptions, properties, and interpretation.
- **The estimator** — Which estimator do you use? Describe the assumptions used to derive the estimator. State the estimator (either equation or the numerical problem that the estimator solves).
- **Properties of the estimator** — What are the properties of the estimator? Under which assumptions? How can you do inference? How can you test restrictions on the model?

*No need to describe the misspecification tests. But — of course — it is important that you apply the misspecification tests correctly in the empirical analysis.*

---

## The Econometric Theory — Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**

- Write the general model for the **specific problem you consider!**
- Present the model equation(s) first, then discuss the model. Much easier to follow!
- Make sure you define all variables and parameters.
- Explain if the economic theory you consider imposes restrictions on the econometric model (e.g., restrictions on certain parameters).

**Don't:**

- Include theory that is not relevant.
- Explain the estimator you use before defining the model.
- Copy-paste general expressions from the lecture notes (e.g., general expression for estimator β̂ given *y*ₜ and *x*ₜ, while the presented model is for *c*ₜ and *y*ₜ).

---

## Description of the Data

> Graphs of the data and relevant transformations must be presented. The data source should be mentioned.

**What to include:**

- What data is it? Sources. Sample period. Data frequency.
- Graphs of the variables; plus relevant transformations.
- Short description of the properties of the data. Do you see anything in the graphs that could be relevant for the choice of econometric model and for the empirical analysis?

**Do:**

- Comment on what might be important from a statistical point of view (e.g. does *y*ₜ appear stationary? Is it upward trending?) and on visible potentially important historical events.
- Keep this part relatively short.

**Don't:**

- *"Figure 1 shows that the variable xₜ went up, then down, then up a bit, then flat, then down a bit..."*

## The Empirical Analysis: Assessment Criteria

> The empirical analysis must include the following:

- A presentation and discussion of the relevant empirical results, so that the reader is able to understand and reproduce the steps carried out in the process as well as the conclusions made. The results should be presented in suitably formatted tables. *Irrelevant information should be left out of the tables.*
- A description of the model selection process based on a general-to-specific approach, information criteria, or both.
- A discussion of whether the assumptions for consistent estimation and valid inference are satisfied for the estimated models. Specifically, this includes misspecification testing, which must be presented and discussed before statistical testing is carried out.
- A clear conclusion to the main question and a discussion of the limitations of the approach used to reach the conclusion.

---

## The Empirical Analysis

**What to include:**

- **Empirical results** — Results in formatted tables.
- **Model selection** — Where do you start? Why? Which tests do you carry out and what is their conclusion? How do you proceed? Why? Where do you end? What is your preferred model? Why?
- **Misspecification tests and link to econometric theory** — Are the assumptions of the theoretical model fulfilled for the estimated model? If not, what are the consequences?
- **Interpretation of the empirical results** — Interpret the results. What are the implications? Are the results as expected or surprising?

---

## The Empirical Analysis — Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**

- Always argue for your choices! One of the most important parts of the assignment.
- Explain what you do and present the relevant results, so that your empirical analysis can be reproduced.
- Refer to the equations in the econometric theory section instead of repeating the equations for all estimated models.
- Discuss misspecification tests before statistical testing.
- Clearly explain the conclusion to the main question of the analysis.

**Don't:**

- Repeat all model equations from the econometric theory section.
- Exclude relevant results (e.g., only report estimates for some model parameters).
- Only report results for the final model you end up with.
- Forget to discuss misspecification tests.

---

## Discussion and Conclusion

**What to include:**

- **Limitations of the econometric approach** — What are the weaknesses of the approach? Could another approach have been used? Do you get different results if you did something different (e.g., if you dropped one variable, excluded dummy-variables)?
- **Comparison of results** — Compare with other results, if they are available.
- **Concluding remarks** — Briefly summarize your conclusion to the main question. Link back to introduction. *(Might be skipped altogether as this is already in the introduction.)*

---

## Presentation of the Results

- If you estimate several different model specifications along the way, please include the results for the relevant ones.
- Tables and figures must be nicely formatted and have numbers (so you can reference them in the text) and a caption (so they are self-explanatory). **Do not copy and paste the output from OxMetrics!**
- For example, make a nice table with estimates from four different models (with different restrictions imposed or not imposed) in four columns.
- Do not include irrelevant information in the table. Include the estimated coefficients, but also standard errors and other relevant information for the model (misspecification tests, the log-likelihood, the estimated residual variance, etc.).
- Gives the reader an understanding of the process you went through and the chance to evaluate the choices you have made.
