\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This paper analyzes the dynamic relationship between geopolitical risk, stock returns, and U.S. economic activity using a monthly VAR model. The central question is whether geopolitical risk drives financial and real activity dynamics, whether reverse predictive channels are present, and whether there is evidence of contemporaneous comovement.

The preferred model is a reduced-form VAR selected by information criteria and validated through misspecification diagnostics. In baseline Gaussian Wald tests, stock returns strongly predict industrial production growth, while geopolitical risk does not significantly predict either stock returns or production growth in the three-variable system. Because heteroskedasticity and non-normality are present, I also report HAC-robust tests for key restrictions. Under robust inference, the conclusion that geopolitical risk has weak predictive power is unchanged, while the stock-return predictive channel becomes less statistically sharp.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Description of the Data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The sample contains monthly observations from January 1985 to December 2025 (492 usable observations). The variables and transformations are:
\begin{align*}
g_t &= \log(GPR_t), \\
y_t &= \log(INDPRO_t)-\log(INDPRO_{t-1}), \\
s_t &= \log\left(1+\frac{Mkt_t}{100}\right).
\end{align*}
Thus, $y_t$ is monthly industrial production growth and $s_t$ is continuously compounded monthly stock return.

Figure~\ref{fig:series} displays the three series with major event windows (9/11, COVID-19, 2022 war shock, and 2023--2024 Middle East escalation). The data show large crisis outliers, especially in $y_t$ during early 2020, and visibly non-Gaussian tail behavior.

\section{Econometric Theory}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Define $Z_t=(g_t,s_t,y_t)'$. The reduced-form VAR($k$) is
\begin{equation}
Z_t = \mu + \Pi_1 Z_{t-1} + \cdots + \Pi_k Z_{t-k} + e_t,
\end{equation}
with $E[e_t\mid\mathcal{I}_{t-1}] = 0$ and $E[e_t e_t'] = \Omega$.

Under stationarity and standard regularity conditions, equation-by-equation OLS coincides with Gaussian MLE in the VAR. Asymptotically, coefficient estimators are normal, so Wald/LR tests are valid for linear restrictions. Stability requires roots of the companion representation outside the unit circle.

To test predictive causality, I test block exclusion restrictions in each equation. For example, no Granger causality from $g$ to $s$ in VAR($k$) is
\begin{equation}
H_0: \text{all } k \text{ coefficients on } g_{t-j} \text{ in the } s_t \text{ equation are zero.}
\end{equation}
Under correct specification, Wald statistics are asymptotically $\chi^2$.

Because diagnostics indicate heteroskedasticity and non-normality, robust covariance estimation is relevant for inference. I therefore report HAC-based Wald tests for key restrictions in addition to baseline VAR Wald tests.

For dynamic interpretation of shocks, I use orthogonalized impulse responses from a Cholesky factorization with ordering $(g,s,y)$, imposing that geopolitical risk does not respond contemporaneously within month to the other two variables.

\subsection{Modeling Progress and Model Selection}

I estimate VAR($k$) for $k=1,2,3,4$ and compare AIC, BIC, and HQIC. BIC selects VAR(1), while AIC is slightly lower at VAR(3). Following a parsimonious strategy, I choose VAR(1) as preferred and then evaluate misspecification before conducting inferential interpretation.

\subsection{Misspecification and Consequences for Inference}

Table~\ref{tab:diagnostics} reports equation-level diagnostics. Residual autocorrelation is borderline in the $g_t$ equation and stronger in the $y_t$ equation at lag 12. ARCH-LM rejects homoskedasticity for $s_t$ and $y_t$, and Jarque-Bera rejects normality in all equations.

\input{tables/table2_diagnostics.tex}

These outcomes imply that standard Gaussian Wald significance can be misleading in finite samples. Estimation of dynamic coefficients remains informative, but inference should be interpreted with caution and supplemented by robust covariance methods.

Contemporaneous dependence is assessed by residual correlations. The estimated values are moderate: $\text{corr}(e_g,e_s)\approx -0.106$, $\text{corr}(e_g,e_y)\approx 0.004$, and $\text{corr}(e_s,e_y)\approx -0.058$.

\subsection{Predictive Channels: Baseline and Robust Tests}

Baseline VAR Wald tests show no Granger-causality from $g$ to $s$ or $y$, and strong evidence for $s\rightarrow y$.

\input{tables/table3_granger.tex}

Given misspecification evidence, I also test key restrictions using HAC covariance in equation-by-equation regressions (Table~\ref{tab:granger_hac}). The robust tests confirm weak predictive content from $g$ to both $s$ and $y$. The $s\rightarrow y$ channel is no longer significant at 5\% under HAC, which indicates that the baseline significance partly reflects non-constant volatility.

Figure~\ref{fig:irf_main} shows orthogonalized IRFs from the preferred VAR(1). A one-standard-deviation GPR shock reduces stock returns on impact (around $-0.0048$ in log-return units), then decays quickly. The response of production growth is small and close to zero.

\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The approach is transparent and reproducible, and it directly targets the assignment question. At the same time, three limitations are important.

First, the three-variable VAR is intentionally parsimonious and cannot represent the broader macro-financial transmission channels emphasized in larger GPR systems. Omitted variables may attenuate direct GPR effects.

Second, misspecification tests indicate heteroskedastic and heavy-tailed residuals, especially around crisis episodes. This weakens reliance on Gaussian finite-sample p-values and motivates robust inference.

Third, Cholesky identification is a maintained assumption. Ordering GPR first is economically plausible and consistent with the course framing, but contemporaneous restrictions are not directly testable.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Using monthly data from 1985--2025, I estimate a parsimonious VAR for geopolitical risk, stock returns, and U.S. industrial production growth. The preferred model is VAR(1) by BIC.

The main finding is that lagged geopolitical risk does not robustly predict stock returns or production growth in this setup. Stock returns contain predictive information for production growth in baseline tests, but this channel becomes less statistically precise under HAC-robust inference. Orthogonalized IRFs still indicate an immediate negative stock-market reaction to a GPR shock, with limited persistence and weak direct real-activity response.

Overall, the evidence suggests that in this compact monthly system, financial variables remain the strongest short-run transmission channel, while direct predictive effects of aggregate GPR on activity are modest.
