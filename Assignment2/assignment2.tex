\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This paper analyzes the dynamic relationship between geopolitical risk, stock returns, and U.S. economic activity using a monthly VAR model. The central question is whether geopolitical risk drives financial and real activity dynamics, whether reverse predictive channels are present, and whether there is evidence of contemporaneous comovement.

The preferred model is a reduced-form VAR selected by information criteria and validated through misspecification diagnostics. Baseline Gaussian Wald tests show no Granger causality from geopolitical risk to either stock returns or production growth, and baseline evidence for $s\rightarrow y$. Because residual diagnostics indicate heteroskedasticity and non-normality, I report HAC-robust Wald tests as the main inferential benchmark. Under robust inference, the conclusion that geopolitical risk has weak predictive power is unchanged, while the $s\rightarrow y$ channel is no longer significant at 5\%.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Description of the Data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The sample contains monthly observations from January 1985 to December 2025 (492 usable observations). The variables and transformations are:
\begin{align*}
g_t &= \log(GPR_t), \\
y_t &= \log(INDPRO_t)-\log(INDPRO_{t-1}), \\
s_t &= \log\left(1+\frac{Mkt_t}{100}\right).
\end{align*}
Thus, $y_t$ is monthly industrial production growth and $s_t$ is continuously compounded monthly stock return.

Data sources are the Caldara-Iacoviello geopolitical risk database (GPR), FRED (INDPRO), and the Fama-French data library (Mkt).

Figure~\ref{fig:series} displays the three series with major event windows (9/11, COVID-19, 2022 war shock, and 2023--2024 Middle East escalation). The data show large crisis outliers, especially in $y_t$ during early 2020, and visibly non-Gaussian tail behavior.
The full graph is reported in Appendix Figure A1.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Econometric Theory}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Define $Z_t=(g_t,s_t,y_t)'$. The reduced-form VAR($k$) is
\begin{equation}
Z_t = \mu + \Pi_1 Z_{t-1} + \cdots + \Pi_k Z_{t-k} + e_t,
\end{equation}
with $E[e_t\mid\mathcal{I}_{t-1}] = 0$ and $E[e_t e_t'] = \Omega$.

Under stationarity and standard regularity conditions, equation-by-equation OLS coincides with Gaussian MLE in the VAR. Asymptotically, coefficient estimators are normal, so Wald/LR tests are valid for linear restrictions. Stability requires roots of the companion representation outside the unit circle.

To test predictive causality, I test block exclusion restrictions in each equation. For example, no Granger causality from $g$ to $s$ in VAR($k$) is
\begin{equation}
H_0: \text{all } k \text{ coefficients on } g_{t-j} \text{ in the } s_t \text{ equation are zero.}
\end{equation}
Under correct specification, Wald statistics are asymptotically $\chi^2$.

Misspecification matters for inference: with heteroskedastic or serially dependent residuals, conventional Gaussian covariance estimates are not reliable, so baseline Wald p-values can be distorted in finite samples. I therefore report HAC-based Wald tests for key restrictions and use these as the primary inference when baseline and robust conclusions differ.

For dynamic interpretation of shocks, I use orthogonalized impulse responses from a Cholesky factorization with ordering $(g,s,y)$, imposing that geopolitical risk does not respond contemporaneously within month to the other two variables. This is a maintained identifying assumption, so IRFs are interpreted conditionally on that ordering.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Empirical Analysis}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Modeling Progress and Model Selection}

I estimate VAR($k$) for $k=1,2,3,4$ and compare AIC, BIC, and HQIC. BIC selects VAR(1), while AIC is slightly lower at VAR(3). Because each extra lag adds nine slope coefficients in this three-variable system, I follow BIC and retain VAR(1) as preferred, then evaluate misspecification before inference. A short VAR(2) sensitivity check is discussed in the robustness paragraph.
Lag-selection numbers are summarized in Appendix Table A1.

\subsection{Misspecification and Consequences for Inference}

Equation-level diagnostics indicate residual autocorrelation in parts of the system and strong non-normality. Residual autocorrelation is borderline in the $g_t$ equation and stronger in the $y_t$ equation at lag 12. ARCH-LM rejects homoskedasticity for $s_t$ and $y_t$, and Jarque-Bera rejects normality in all equations.
Compact p-value diagnostics are shown in Appendix Table A2.

These outcomes imply that standard Gaussian Wald significance can be misleading in finite samples. Estimation of dynamic coefficients remains informative, but formal hypothesis testing should rely on robust covariance methods. I therefore treat HAC-based Wald tests as the main inferential results.

Contemporaneous dependence is assessed by residual correlations. The estimated values are moderate: $\text{corr}(e_g,e_s)\approx -0.106$, $\text{corr}(e_g,e_y)\approx 0.004$, and $\text{corr}(e_s,e_y)\approx -0.058$. These correlations motivate caution in structural interpretation, since recursive contemporaneous restrictions are not directly testable.

\subsection{Predictive Channels: Baseline and Robust Tests}

Baseline VAR Wald tests show no Granger-causality from $g$ to $s$ or $y$, and baseline evidence for $s\rightarrow y$.

Given misspecification evidence, I also test key restrictions using HAC covariance in equation-by-equation regressions. The robust tests confirm weak predictive content from $g$ to both $s$ and $y$. The $s\rightarrow y$ channel is no longer significant at 5\% under HAC, which indicates that the baseline significance partly reflects non-constant volatility.
Baseline and HAC p-values are summarized in Appendix Table A3.

\subsection{Impulse Responses and Sample Robustness}

Figure~\ref{fig:irf_main} shows orthogonalized IRFs from the preferred VAR(1). Under the maintained Cholesky ordering, a one-standard-deviation GPR shock reduces stock returns on impact (around $-0.0048$ in log-return units), then decays quickly. The response of production growth is small and close to zero.
The complete IRF panel is reported in Appendix Figure A2.

I re-estimate the model on two alternative samples: post-9/11 only, and a sample excluding 2020--2024; I also check VAR(2) briefly as a sensitivity analysis. The qualitative message is unchanged: $g\rightarrow s$ and $g\rightarrow y$ predictive channels remain weak, while financial dynamics continue to be the dominant short-run channel.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The approach is transparent and reproducible, and it directly targets the assignment question. At the same time, three limitations are important.

First, the three-variable VAR is intentionally parsimonious and cannot represent the broader macro-financial transmission channels emphasized in larger GPR systems. Omitted variables may attenuate direct GPR effects.

Second, misspecification tests indicate heteroskedastic and heavy-tailed residuals, especially around crisis episodes. This weakens reliance on Gaussian finite-sample p-values and motivates robust inference.

Third, Cholesky identification is a maintained assumption. Ordering GPR first is economically plausible and consistent with the course framing, but contemporaneous restrictions are not directly testable.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Using monthly data from 1985--2025, I estimate a parsimonious VAR for geopolitical risk, stock returns, and U.S. industrial production growth. The preferred model is VAR(1) by BIC.

The main finding is that lagged geopolitical risk does not robustly predict stock returns or production growth in this setup. Stock returns contain predictive information for production growth in baseline tests, but this channel is not significant at 5\% under HAC-robust inference. Orthogonalized IRFs, interpreted conditionally on the Cholesky ordering, still indicate an immediate negative stock-market reaction to a GPR shock, with limited persistence and weak direct real-activity response.

Overall, the evidence suggests that in this compact monthly system, financial variables remain the strongest short-run transmission channel, while direct predictive effects of aggregate GPR on activity are modest.
