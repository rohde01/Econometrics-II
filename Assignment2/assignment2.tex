\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This paper analyzes the dynamic relationship between geopolitical risk, stock returns, and U.S. economic activity using a monthly VAR model. The central question is whether geopolitical risk drives financial and real activity dynamics, whether reverse predictive channels are present, and whether there is evidence of contemporaneous comovement.
\newline
\newline
The preferred model is a reduced-form VAR selected by information criteria and validated through misspecification diagnostics. Baseline Gaussian Wald tests show no Granger causality from geopolitical risk to either stock returns or production growth, and baseline evidence for $s\rightarrow y$. Because residual diagnostics indicate heteroskedasticity and non-normality, we report HAC-robust Wald tests as the main inferential benchmark. Under robust inference, the conclusion that geopolitical risk has weak predictive power is unchanged, while the $s\rightarrow y$ channel is no longer significant at 5\%.

\section{Description of the Data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The sample contains monthly observations from January 1985 to December 2025 (492 usable observations). The variables and transformations are:
\begin{align*}
g_t &= \log(GPR_t), \\
y_t &= \log(INDPRO_t)-\log(INDPRO_{t-1}), \\
s_t &= \log\left(1+\frac{Mkt_t}{100}\right).
\end{align*}
Thus, $y_t$ is monthly industrial production growth and $s_t$ is continuously compounded monthly stock return.
\newline
Data sources are the Caldara-Iacoviello geopolitical risk database (GPR), FRED (INDPRO), and the Fama-French data library (Mkt).
\newline
\newline
Figure~\ref{fig:series} displays the three series with major event windows (9/11, COVID-19, 2022 war shock, and 2023--2024 Middle East escalation). The data show large crisis outliers, especially in $y_t$ during early 2020, and visibly non-Gaussian tail behavior.

\section{Econometric Theory}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The assignment studies whether geopolitical risk helps predict stock returns and real activity, and whether the relationship is mainly lagged (predictive) or contemporaneous. To analyze this, we model the three monthly variables jointly as
\[
Z_t=(g_t,s_t,y_t)' ,
\]
where \(g_t\) is log geopolitical risk, \(s_t\) is log stock return, and \(y_t\) is industrial production growth.
\newline
\newline
The baseline model is a reduced-form VAR(\(k\)):
\begin{equation}
Z_t = \mu + \Pi_1 Z_{t-1} + \cdots + \Pi_k Z_{t-k} + e_t ,
\end{equation}
with innovations satisfying
\[
E(e_t \mid \mathcal I_{t-1})=0,\qquad
E(e_t e_t')=\Omega,\qquad
E(e_t e_{t-j}')=0\ \text{for } j\ge 1,
\]
and \(\Omega\) symmetric positive definite. Thus, each variable is explained by its own lags and lags of the other variables, while contemporaneous comovement is summarized by \(\Omega\).
\newline
\newline
A key requirement for standard VAR inference is stability (covariance stationarity). For a VAR(\(k\)), this is checked in companion form: all eigenvalues must lie strictly inside the unit circle (equivalently, all roots of the characteristic polynomial lie outside the unit circle). Under stability and standard regularity conditions, equation-by-equation OLS is numerically identical to Gaussian MLE in the VAR, and
\[
\sqrt{T}\,\mathrm{vec}\!\left(\hat\Phi-\Phi\right)\xrightarrow{d}N(0,V),
\]
so Wald and LR tests are asymptotically valid for linear restrictions.
\newline
\newline
To test predictive (Granger) causality, we use block exclusion restrictions. For example, ``\(g\) does not Granger-cause \(s\)'' in VAR(\(k\)) is
\begin{equation}
H_0:\ \Pi_{s,g,1}=\Pi_{s,g,2}=\cdots=\Pi_{s,g,k}=0,
\end{equation}
i.e., all coefficients on \(g_{t-j}\) in the \(s_t\)-equation are jointly zero. Under correct specification, the Wald statistic is asymptotically \(\chi^2(m)\), where \(m\) is the number of restrictions (here \(m=k\)). Granger causality is about incremental forecasting power and does \emph{not} identify contemporaneous causality.
\newline
\newline
Misspecification affects inference: if residuals are heteroskedastic or non-Gaussian, conventional (model-based) covariance estimates can understate uncertainty. Following the course-note guidance, we therefore complement baseline Gaussian Wald tests with HAC-robust Wald tests for key restrictions and treat robust inference as primary whenever conclusions differ.
\newline
\newline
For dynamic interpretation of shocks, we report impulse-response functions (IRFs). Reduced-form shocks are generally correlated, so economically interpretable IRFs require orthogonalization. We use a recursive (Cholesky) identification with ordering \((g,s,y)\), which imposes that geopolitical risk is predetermined within the month relative to stock returns and production growth. Hence, IRFs are interpreted conditionally on this maintained ordering assumption; in particular, contemporaneous feedback to \(g_t\) within the month is ruled out by construction.

\subsection{Misspecification and Consequences for Inference}

Equation-level diagnostics indicate residual autocorrelation in parts of the system and strong non-normality. Residual autocorrelation is borderline in the $g_t$ equation and stronger in the $y_t$ equation at lag 12. ARCH-LM rejects homoskedasticity for $s_t$ and $y_t$, and Jarque-Bera rejects normality in all equations.
Compact p-value diagnostics are shown in Table~\ref{tab:diag_compact}.

\input{tables/table7_diag_compact.tex}


These outcomes imply that standard Gaussian Wald significance can be misleading in finite samples. Estimation of dynamic coefficients remains informative, but formal hypothesis testing should rely on robust covariance methods. We therefore treat HAC-based Wald tests as the main inferential results.
\newline
\newline
Contemporaneous dependence is assessed by residual correlations. The estimated values are moderate: $\text{corr}(e_g,e_s)\approx -0.106$, $\text{corr}(e_g,e_y)\approx 0.004$, and $\text{corr}(e_s,e_y)\approx -0.058$. These correlations motivate caution in structural interpretation, since recursive contemporaneous restrictions are not directly testable.

\subsection{Predictive Channels: Baseline and Robust Tests}

Baseline VAR Wald tests show no Granger-causality from $g$ to $s$ or $y$, and baseline evidence for $s\rightarrow y$.
\newline
Given misspecification evidence, we also test key restrictions using HAC covariance in equation-by-equation regressions. The robust tests confirm weak predictive content from $g$ to both $s$ and $y$. The $s\rightarrow y$ channel is no longer significant at 5\% under HAC, which indicates that the baseline significance partly reflects non-constant volatility.

\section{Discussion}

The empirical strategy is transparent and closely aligned with the assignment objective of separating predictive dynamics from contemporaneous comovement in a multivariate setting. The preferred VAR(1) provides a clear and interpretable benchmark.
\newline
A key strength is consistency across methods: model selection, diagnostics, Granger-causality tests, and IRFs all point to the same qualitative conclusion. Lagged geopolitical risk has limited predictive power for stock returns and industrial production growth in this specification.
\newline
\newline
At the same time, three limitations are important. First, the three-variable VAR is intentionally parsimonious and cannot capture all macro-financial transmission channels emphasized in larger GPR systems; omitted variables may attenuate direct GPR effects.
\newline
Second, misspecification tests indicate heteroskedasticity and non-normal residuals, especially during crisis periods. This weakens reliance on conventional Gaussian finite-sample p-values and motivates robust inference as the primary benchmark.
\newline
Third, orthogonalized IRFs rely on the maintained Cholesky ordering $(g,s,y)$. Ordering GPR first is economically plausible at monthly frequency, but contemporaneous restrictions are not directly testable, so structural interpretation should remain cautious.
\newline
\newline
Overall, the evidence suggests that in this compact monthly system, financial variables are the main short-run transmission channel, while direct predictive effects of aggregate geopolitical risk on real activity are modest.

\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Using monthly U.S. data from 1985--2025, we estimate a parsimonious VAR for geopolitical risk, stock returns, and industrial production growth. Lag-order criteria point to a short dynamic structure, and we therefore use VAR(1) as the preferred specification.
\newline
Our main finding is that lagged geopolitical risk does not provide robust predictive content for either stock returns or production growth in this setup. This conclusion is stable across baseline Granger-type tests and HAC-robust inference, despite evidence of heteroskedasticity and non-normal residuals.
\newline
Baseline results indicate a predictive channel from stock returns to production growth, but this channel is not significant at the 5\% level once robust covariance estimation is applied. This difference between baseline and robust inference suggests that finite-sample significance can be sensitive to residual volatility patterns.
\newline
Orthogonalized impulse responses, interpreted conditional on the Cholesky ordering $(g,s,y)$, show an immediate negative response of stock returns to a GPR shock that decays relatively quickly. The direct response of production growth is small and close to zero over the horizon considered, consistent with limited short-run real-activity transmission in this compact system.
\newline
Robustness checks based on alternative samples (post-9/11 and exclusion of 2020--2024) do not alter the qualitative conclusions. The overall picture is therefore that financial variables remain the dominant short-run adjustment margin, while direct predictive effects of aggregate geopolitical risk on real activity are modest.
\newline
Taken together, the evidence supports a cautious interpretation: geopolitical risk is economically relevant for short-run financial conditions, but its direct predictive role for monthly real activity is weak in this three-variable VAR framework.
